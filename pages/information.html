What is Scoliosis?

A sideways curve of the spine - often shaped like an "S" or "C" when viewed from behind.

  • Usually appears between ages 10-15
  • Affects about 2-3% of people worldwide
  • More common in females (about 3:1 ratio)

Cobb Angle

The standard X-ray measure of how large the spinal curve is.

  1. Find the most tilted vertebra at the top and bottom of the curve
  2. Draw lines along the endplates of those vertebrae
  3. The angle between the lines is the Cobb angle

Severity Levels

Classification based on Cobb angle - guides treatment planning.

Level Cobb Angle Typical Care
Normal < 10° No treatment needed
Mild 10° - 25° Check-ups every 4-6 months
Moderate 25° - 40° Bracing + physiotherapy
Severe > 40° Surgery may be required

Types of Scoliosis

Four main categories by cause.

Idiopathic ~80% of cases

Unknown cause; most common in adolescents (AIS).

Congenital From birth

Spine forms incorrectly during fetal growth.

Neuromuscular Linked condition

With cerebral palsy, muscular dystrophy, or similar.

Degenerative Adults

Disc wear and spine changes in older age.

Treatment Options

Depends on Cobb angle, age, and how fast the curve changes.

Cobb Angle Treatment
10° - 25° Observation & exercise
20° - 40° Physiotherapy (e.g. Schroth)
25° - 45° Spinal bracing (TLSO, etc.)
40°+ Surgical evaluation

When to See a Doctor

Book an appointment if you notice:

  • Uneven shoulders or one hip higher
  • Rib hump when bending forward
  • Persistent back pain (especially in teens)
  • Breathing difficulty
  • Clothes fitting unevenly
